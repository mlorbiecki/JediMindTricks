In this command we see that Sex is in a factor form (ie. Male and Female) and Height is in a numeric form (1,2,3,4…etc) This is important for us to know since we cannot take a mean of a factor variable. We can convert these variables into different types using specific commands. as.character(dataset$variablename) for instance will change whatever variable we are choosing to be understood as a factor. as.numeric(dataset$variablename) for numeric as.factor(dataset$variablename) for factors. In our example a command that would change the sex variable from factor to character would look like so: as.character(survey$Sex)

We also have the ability to add new variables to our data set. We can do this easily with the pulldown menu at the top by going to “Data” > “Manage Variables In Active Data Set” > “Compute New Variable.” A window should pop up. Lets use NWWR.Hnd as the name of our new variable we want to create. Put that name in for the variable name. Under the expression to compute, type in “(Wr.Hnd.5)+(2 NW.Hnd)” What does this mean? Our new variable is equal to half the value of Wr.Hnd added to twice the value of NW.Hnd. Your command should look the same as bellow.

> survey$NWWR.Hnd <- with(survey, (Wr.Hnd*.5)+(2* NW.Hnd))

Let us go one step further and do a Chi Squared test against our hypotheses.Lets say our hypothesis were as follows: 70% are non smokers 10% are heavy smokers 10% are occasional smokers 10% are regular smokers To test this hyposthesis go to “Statistics” > “Summaries” > “Frequency Distributions”. This time however make sure you select in the check box that says “Chi Squared Goodness to Fit” In the next window lets insert our hypothesis as .1 for heavy, .7 for never, .1 for occas, and .1 for regul.

> local({
+   .Table <- with(survey, table(Smoke))
+   cat("\ncounts:\n")
+   print(.Table)
+   cat("\npercentages:\n")
+   print(round(100*.Table/sum(.Table), 2))
+   .Probs <- c(0.1,0.7,0.1,0.1) 
+   chisq.test(.Table, p=.Probs)
+ })


counts:
Smoke
Heavy Never Occas Regul 
   11   189    19    17 

percentages:
Smoke
Heavy Never Occas Regul 
 4.66 80.08  8.05  7.20 


    Chi-squared test for given probabilities

data:  .Table
X-squared = 12.898, df = 3, p-value = 0.004862

P-value is the propability of the chance of a type one error in our analysis. So in this case our type one error is 0.004% As long as this P value is below .01, we can reject our null hypothesis. Since 0.004% is lower than 0.01%, we can say that our Hypothesis is not supported by our description. In laymans terms, we were wrong about our hypothesis.

Numerica Variable Analysis

Let us now look at a numeric variable for analysis. Let us take the numeric summary of the height variable by going to “Statistics” > “Summaries” > “Numerical Summaries” Choose the “Height” variable and click ok. Below are the commands for this. We can now see that the mean of the height is 172.3809

> numSummary(survey[,"Height"], statistics=c("mean", "sd", "IQR", "quantiles"), quantiles=c(0,.25,.5,.75,1))

     mean       sd IQR  0% 25% 50% 75% 100%   n NA

What if we wanted to perform a one-sample T-test for our mean? Well we can do that as well! Go to “Statistics” > “Mean” > “Single Sample T-test”. A box will now appear. Choose your “Height” variable. Insert “175” for your null hypothesis. Insert “.99” for our confidence level. Then click ok. Below is the command for this.

> with(survey, (t.test(Height, alternative='two.sided', mu=175, conf.level=.99)))


    One Sample t-test

data:  Height
t = -3.8451, df = 208, p-value = 0.0001602
alternative hypothesis: true mean is not equal to 175
99 percent confidence interval:
 170.6100 174.1517
sample estimates:
mean of x 
 172.3809

We can see that we have a new P value of “0.0001602.” This is good for our alternate hypothesis of the true mean being less than 175 since our p value allows us to reject our null hypothesis. Our true mean is less than 175.
